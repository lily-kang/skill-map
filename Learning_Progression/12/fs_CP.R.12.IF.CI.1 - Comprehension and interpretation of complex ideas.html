Domain Level Expectations
The foundations for developing close-reading skills begin when students listen for key details and engage with informational text read aloud.With support, students build on foundations as they begin to read and engage with text, applying basic reading strategies, asking and answering questions, making connections and simple comparisons, and identifying a topic, key ideas, and information.As students continue to listen to and begin to read texts on their own, they develop an understanding of the main points and key details as well as how to apply reading strategies.As students continue to build fluency and begin to read informational texts to gain content knowledge, they draw simple conclusions, identify main ideas and key details, describe relationships between ideas (cause/effect, order of events), and distinguish fact from opinion. As students begin to read more diverse texts to expand their knowledge of different subjects, they identify main ideas, explaining how textual details support them. They identify opinions, describe cause-and-effect relationships, make predictions, and draw simple conclusions, using evidence and illustrations from the text.As students engage in domain-specific learning in multiple subjects, they gain information through close reading, examining main ideas and supporting details. They explain the text's meaning and support their explanations and inferences with textual details and examples.Students continue to gain greater understanding of historical, scientific, and technical texts by grasping key ideas and details and making inferences, citing accurate information from the text.Students deepen understanding of subjects by reading a variety of informational texts, applying independent reading strategies, and integrating types of information. They determine how ideas and information are introduced and developed, citing evidence to support analysis and conclusions. As students encounter more complex domain-specific informational texts, they use organizational patterns as guides for comprehension. They analyze the development of central ideas and cite textual evidence to support analysis.As students encounter increasingly complex informational texts and technical processes, they are able to automatically monitor their comprehension as well as synthesize information, explain development of central ideas, and draw conclusions, weighing evidence to support their analysis.Students draw larger conclusions about informational texts based on their analysis and support their conclusions with strong, thorough evidence. They analyze the author's development of the central ideas and understand complex technical or scientific processes, applying reading strategies with automaticity.To understand the implications of complex informational texts, students analyze how the central idea and author's analysis emerge and are shaped and refined, drawing larger conclusions about the text's meaning or significance, citing strong and thorough evidence, and evaluating conflicting information.Students read a wide variety of sophisticated informational and technical texts. They analyze the interaction and development of central ideas and sequences of events. Students support conclusions with thorough evidence, noting where texts leave matters uncertain.Students display a full range of analytical tools which they apply to many challenging informational texts, synthesizing a text's ideas to create complex, comprehensive, well-supported analyses that evaluate the development of the author's thesis, reveal ambiguities, and explain interactions among ideas.

Prerequisite Skills
Grade 11 - Analyze a complex set of ideas or sequence of events in an informational text in order to explain relationships, including how ideas, individuals, or events interact and develop (e.g., contributing factors, degrees of influence)Grade 11 - Explain and interpret the thesis or central idea of a complex informational or technical text and how it is supported and developedGrade 11 - Draw conclusions about the meaning and significance of an informational text based on analysis and evaluation of evidence from the text or from secondary sources to support conclusionsGrade 11 - Select textual evidence based on evaluation of its strength, completeness, and relevance to the analysis of an informational text, noting where the text leaves matters uncertainGrade 11 - Analyze an author's use of language including how an author uses and refines the meaning of key terms over the course of an informational text (e.g., faction in Federalist No. 10)
ELL Support
Preteach content-specific vocabulary and background. For historical figures, such as the examples John Adams and Thomas Jefferson, assign students in small groups to act out various scenes involving the two people.
Content-Area Vocabulary
sequence, connections
Conceptual Knowledge
comprehending a complex sequence of events or set of ideas; recognizing how individuals, ideas, or events relate and develop throughout a text; understanding how an author develops relationships and ideas over the course of a text
Linguistic Competencies
