Analyze an author's values, beliefs, and assumptions (e.g., philosophical, political), which may be directly stated or implied⚡ Focus Skill

Short Skill Name
Analyze an author's values and beliefs
Skill Area
Author's Purpose and Perspective
Domains
Organization, Purpose, and Language Use
Domain Level Expectations
With support, students begin to understand the different purposes for reading informational texts of various topics, identifying the texts’ authors and illustrators as a foundation for early reading comprehension skills.To develop reading comprehension, students become familiar with a range of informational texts, identifying the roles of both author and illustrator and identifying the author's purpose for writing, while also using text features such as titles and illustrations to make predictions.Students discern general differences in printed and digital informational material, noting the role of various text features and closely examining the meaning and effects of word choices to develop greater comprehension of the author's viewpoints and purpose.To engage more fully in their reading, students further explore the purposes and features of informational texts and media as they examine sequential order, figurative language, and how authors use language to create an effect for readers.As students develop the ability to seek information from informational texts, they interpret the author’s purpose and use of sensory language, sequential events and steps, visually presented information, and the overall structure and characteristics of nonfictional genres.To further develop comprehension of informational texts, students continue to analyze the structure, visual presentation of text features, and author’s purpose in order to correctly interpret what is read.Students identify and analyze common structures and language used in informational texts and media, determining the author’s purpose and connections between ideas and making comparisons between digital and printed texts, enabling them to further develop their overall reading comprehension and engagement.To engage fully with nonfiction, students’ analysis of informational text expands to include making comparisons between the various forms (argument pieces, autobiographies) and examining relationships between organizational structure and the author or between the larger ideas of a text.To establish a framework for understanding different works of nonfiction and authors' perspectives, students continue to analyze the characteristics and devices used, such as sequence, graphical components, and the effects of language.Students understand in greater depth how to use informational texts as they continue to analyze how language and other techniques establish an author’s style and voice, and they analyze how presentation of information affects the overall message and impact of a text or media message.To navigate and make full use of informational works, including those with varying accounts of the same subject, students analyze more complex texts and media with a focus on the interrelationship of an author’s background, word choice, and message.As they make more sophisticated interpretations of a variety of nonfictional works, students analyze in greater depth how authors impact an audience with background, language, refinement of ideas and processes, and supporting evidence.To make more complex interpretations of nonfictional works and media, students analyze the impact of structure, genre characteristics, text features, and authors’ language choices on a text and how the author’s intended audience is influenced.Students intuitively evaluate the effectiveness of structure, text features, cultural and historical influence, and an author’s use of language and literary devices on informational texts.
Standards
CP.R.11.IF.OP.4 - Author's perspective and effect on audience
Prerequisite Skills
Grade 10 - Select textual evidence based on evaluation of its strength, completeness, and relevance to the analysis of an informational textGrade 10 - Analyze complex or conflicting information and textual details in order to arrive at conclusions about the meaning and significance of an informational textGrade 10 - Identify unstated assumptions on which an author's argument may be based (e.g., assumptions about the reader's prior knowledge, opinions, and values)
ELL Support
Provide graphic organizers to trace the development of an author's argument, and provide sentence stems to analyze the author's values, beliefs, and assumptions (e.g., Because the author argues that ____, we can assume that he/she believes _____.).
Content-Area Vocabulary
argument, values, beliefs, assumptions, explicit, implicit
Conceptual Knowledge
comprehending an argument and recognizing the assumptions, beliefs, and values it is based on
Linguistic Competencies
finding words that are clues to opinions (e.g., should, must, ought, believe, never, always, most)
